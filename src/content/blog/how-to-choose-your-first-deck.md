Walk into a magic shop and you'll find hundreds of decks. Skull-covered, gilded edges, custom pips, themed backs. It's overwhelming. And for a beginner, it's a trap.

Here's the truth: the fancier the deck, the worse it is for learning.

## What Makes a Good Practice Deck

**Handling matters most.** For sleight of hand, you need cards that:
- Fan smoothly and evenly
- Spring back naturally
- Don't stick together or clump

This comes down to **stock quality** and **finish**.

### The Bicycle Standard

For beginners, start with **Bicycle Standard** playing cards (red or blue back). They're:
- Inexpensive (~$4–6)
- Widely available (every grocery store)
- Air-cushion finish for smooth fans
- The industry standard for a reason

Every technique you'll learn was designed around Bicycle-style handling. Card magic references assume you're using a standard Bicycle or equivalent.

### When to Upgrade

Once you're comfortable with basics, consider:

**Tally-Ho (Fan Back or Circle Back)** — slightly stiffer, excellent for fans and flourishes  
**Cartamundi B9 stock** — used in casino decks, very durable  
**Theory11 or Dan & Dave decks** — premium stock, great for one-handed work, pricier

### What to Avoid

- **Novelty/themed decks** for practice (bad stock, inconsistent sizing)
- **Laminated casino decks** (too stiff for fans)
- **Plastic decks** (entirely different feel — only for outdoor/pool performances)
- **Marked decks** before you've learned the non-marked version of a trick

## How Many Decks Do You Need?

Buy four of the same deck at once. Here's why:

1. Cards wear out fast with intensive practice
2. You want consistent handling across all your practice sessions
3. Worn decks handle *better* than new ones for many sleights — break them in intentionally

## The New Deck Shuffle

When you open a new deck, it's sorted by suit and value. Shuffle it thoroughly before practicing:
1. Four overhand shuffles
2. Two riffle shuffles
3. A few cuts

This breaks in the stock and removes the "new deck" stiffness.

---

The deck is just a tool. A great magician can fool you with a beat-up Bicycle from a gas station. Start there. Worry about premium decks when you're performing, not practicing.
